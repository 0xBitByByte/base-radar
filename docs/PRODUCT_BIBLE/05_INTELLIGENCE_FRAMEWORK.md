## Executive Summary

Base Radar is not a data platform, not a dashboard, and not a tracker — it is an Intelligence Platform, and this chapter defines what that word is actually allowed to mean. Every other chapter in this Bible treats "intelligence" as a given: [01. Product Strategy](01_PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md) builds the mission around it, [02. UX Strategy](02_UX_STRATEGY.md) defines how it's presented, [03. Information Architecture](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md) defines where it structurally lives, and [04. Feature Specifications](04_FEATURE_SPECIFICATIONS.md) lists the capabilities that surface it. This chapter is where the word is actually defined — the philosophy, principles, and trust model that separate a genuine Insight from a repackaged fact. [08. Competitive Analysis](08_COMPETITIVE_ANALYSIS.md) already identified this as Base Radar's sharpest point of differentiation: where competitors bolt a conversational AI feature onto a general data lake, Base Radar's intelligence is a consistent, always-on layer applied uniformly to everything it tracks. This chapter is the philosophy that claim has to be true to.

This chapter never defines an algorithm, a model, or a prompt. It defines the rules any algorithm, model, or prompt must obey to be allowed to call its output "intelligence" rather than merely "data."

Every intelligence output in Base Radar answers five questions, in this order:

1. **What happened?** — the observation.
2. **Why did it happen?** — the interpretation.
3. **How important is it?** — the significance.
4. **How confident are we?** — the evidence quality.
5. **What should I do next?** — the optional, never mandatory, implication.

This is the intelligence-production counterpart to [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#ux-philosophy)'s four-question UX contract (What happened? Why does it matter? How confident are we? What should I do next?) — 02's four questions are what a *screen* must be able to answer; these five are what a piece of *intelligence* must be able to answer about itself before it is allowed onto a screen at all. "Why does it matter" on a screen is the surfaced result of resolving both "why did it happen" and "how important is it" here — two separate epistemic questions that 02 deliberately compresses into one for the reader's benefit.

## Intelligence Philosophy

Several pillars below share a name with a principle already established in [01](01_PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md#product-philosophy) or [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#ux-philosophy). Where they do, this is the same value applied to how intelligence is *produced*, not how it's *strategized about* or *displayed* — named consistently, not re-argued.

- **Signal over Noise.** At the production level: only a genuinely meaningful deviation is synthesized into an Insight. Volume of data is never mistaken for depth of understanding.
- **Explain Before Recommend.** A Recommendation's explanation must exist *before* the Recommendation is generated, not written afterward to justify it. If the explanation can't be produced first, the Recommendation isn't ready to exist.
- **Evidence Before Opinion.** No Insight is synthesized without Evidence existing first. The pipeline never works backward from a desired conclusion to evidence that would support it.
- **Trust Before Automation.** Nothing acts autonomously on a Recommendation before the Recommendation's accuracy has been earned through disclosure — consistent with why [04](04_FEATURE_SPECIFICATIONS.md#notifications)'s Automation surfaces are data-only today, never an auto-executed action.
- **Transparency over Certainty.** Base Radar would rather be transparently uncertain than falsely certain — certainty is never manufactured to make an output feel more finished.
- **Confidence over Assumption.** Nothing is treated as true without a stated confidence level attached to it, detailed in the [Confidence Framework](#confidence-framework) below.
- **Actionable over Informational.** Intelligence exists to inform a real decision, not merely to display a fact — the line this chapter draws between being a data platform and being an Intelligence Platform.
- **Context Creates Intelligence.** A raw fact becomes intelligence only once given context — history, a peer comparison, a threshold — never before. This is why [03](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#product-hierarchy)'s Intelligence level sits structurally below Project/Entity rather than being indistinguishable from it.
- **AI Assists Judgment.** Shared root with [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#ux-philosophy). At the production level: nothing generated by this framework is designed to be obeyed — it is designed to be weighed.
- **Never Replace User Thinking.** The intelligence layer's job ends at a fully explained Recommendation. The decision itself always remains the user's, never the Platform's.

## Intelligence Design Principles

Thirteen operating principles translate the philosophy above into standards specific enough to hold any intelligence output accountable.

### 1. No Insight Without Evidence

- **Purpose:** An Insight cannot exist unless real Evidence produced it.
- **Reasoning:** An Insight with no Evidence behind it is an opinion wearing an Insight's clothing.
- **Real-world example:** A Health Score is never assigned without the underlying signals that justify it being retrievable on demand.
- **Expected user outcome:** A user who distrusts a conclusion can always find out what produced it.

### 2. No Recommendation Without Explanation

- **Purpose:** A Recommendation is incomplete, and therefore not shippable, until its Explanation exists alongside it.
- **Reasoning:** A Recommendation without an Explanation asks for obedience; one with an Explanation asks for consideration.
- **Real-world example:** "Worth watching" is never shown without the Evidence that made it worth watching, in the same view.
- **Expected user outcome:** Users evaluate a Recommendation on its merits, never on the Platform's authority alone.

### 3. Confidence Reflects Evidence, Never Optimism

- **Purpose:** A confidence level is a direct function of the [Confidence Framework](#confidence-framework)'s dimensions — never a subjective sense that a conclusion "feels right."
- **Reasoning:** Optimism about a conclusion and evidence for a conclusion are different things; conflating them is how false certainty enters a system.
- **Real-world example:** A single, unconfirmed source producing a striking signal earns a lower confidence level than a routine signal confirmed by several sources.
- **Expected user outcome:** A user learns to trust the confidence level itself, not just the conclusion it's attached to.

### 4. Every Domain Has a Single Scoring Vocabulary

- **Purpose:** Health, Risk, and Confidence are each expressed through one finite set of labels, reused identically across every domain that uses them.
- **Reasoning:** A second, slightly different vocabulary for the same concept is how two true statements start to look like a contradiction.
- **Real-world example:** "Risk: Moderate" means the same thing whether it appears on a Project Page or inside Portfolio synthesis — the same finite vocabulary [03](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#taxonomy) already defines.
- **Expected user outcome:** A label learned once is understood everywhere it reappears.

### 5. Narratives Are Classifications, Not Predictions

- **Purpose:** A Narrative describes a pattern already observed in the evidence — it never forecasts what will happen next.
- **Reasoning:** Classifying the present is a claim this framework can support with Evidence; predicting the future is a claim it cannot.
- **Real-world example:** "Growth" describes an observed trend; it is never presented as a guarantee that growth will continue.
- **Expected user outcome:** Users never mistake a description of now for a promise about later.

### 6. Absence of Data Is Reported, Never Guessed

- **Purpose:** When a real signal genuinely isn't available, the framework says so rather than inferring a plausible-sounding substitute.
- **Reasoning:** A guess dressed as a fact is the single fastest way to destroy the trust this entire framework depends on.
- **Real-world example:** A missing developer-activity signal is reported as unavailable, never silently backfilled with an assumed average.
- **Expected user outcome:** A gap a user is told about is a gap they can work around; a gap they aren't told about is a mistake waiting to surface later.

### 7. Conflicting Evidence Is Disclosed, Not Resolved Silently

- **Purpose:** When two real signals disagree, both are surfaced, along with the disagreement itself.
- **Reasoning:** Silently picking a winner between conflicting signals hides a genuine, useful piece of information: that the picture is unsettled.
- **Real-world example:** A rising price alongside falling developer activity is shown as exactly that tension, never quietly averaged into one falsely tidy read.
- **Expected user outcome:** Users are trusted with genuine ambiguity rather than handed a false, artificial resolution of it.

### 8. Intelligence Decays — Freshness Is Part of Truth

- **Purpose:** How recently a signal was observed is treated as part of what makes it true, not a separate footnote.
- **Reasoning:** A correct answer to yesterday's question is not automatically a correct answer to today's.
- **Real-world example:** A Health Score computed from a week-old snapshot is treated, and labeled, differently from one computed an hour ago.
- **Expected user outcome:** A user never mistakes stale intelligence for current intelligence.

### 9. One Fact, One Interpretation

- **Purpose:** No two intelligence outputs may assign conflicting interpretations to the same underlying fact at the same time.
- **Reasoning:** This is the production-layer source of [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#core-ux-principles)'s "the interface never argues with itself" — that principle is only enforceable on screen because this framework enforces it at the source first.
- **Real-world example:** A single project cannot be "Growth" in one surface and "Declining" in another at the same moment; one interpretation is always canonical.
- **Expected user outcome:** Users never have to reconcile the Platform disagreeing with itself.

### 10. Automation Requires a Human-Legible Trail

- **Purpose:** Anything evaluated automatically must be explainable in exactly the same terms as something a person reviewed manually.
- **Reasoning:** Automation is a production method, not an excuse to lower the bar for explainability.
- **Real-world example:** An Automation result names the rule, the trigger, and the affected project — never just "something happened."
- **Expected user outcome:** Automated and manually-reviewed intelligence are equally trustworthy, because they're held to one standard.

### 11. Context Before Conclusion

- **Purpose:** A conclusion is only reached after its context (history, comparison, expectation) has actually been established, never before.
- **Reasoning:** A conclusion reached before its context exists is a conclusion that context is then forced to justify retroactively.
- **Real-world example:** "+12% this week" is only ever computed once the underlying week of data genuinely exists to support that comparison.
- **Expected user outcome:** Every comparison a user sees is one the framework actually earned, not one assembled after the fact.

### 12. Coverage Gaps Are Named, Not Hidden

- **Purpose:** Where the Platform's Evidence has a genuine blind spot (a domain, a chain, a data type it doesn't yet cover), that gap is stated plainly.
- **Reasoning:** An unstated blind spot is indistinguishable, to a user, from a domain that was checked and found clean.
- **Real-world example:** A Risk classification that couldn't check for one real risk category says so, rather than implying a clean bill of health.
- **Expected user outcome:** A user can tell the difference between "checked and fine" and "not checked."

### 13. Recommendations Never Instruct, Only Inform

- **Purpose:** A Recommendation is always phrased as a consideration for the user to weigh, never a directive to follow.
- **Reasoning:** Instructing implies an authority over the user's decision this framework was never meant to claim.
- **Real-world example:** "Worth watching" and "elevated risk" are Recommendations; "sell now" is not a form any Recommendation in Base Radar takes.
- **Expected user outcome:** A user's own judgment stays the final step in every decision, exactly as [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#ai-experience)'s AI Experience already promises on screen.

### 14. Traceable Intelligence *(Non-Negotiable)*

- **Purpose:** Every intelligence output Base Radar produces — every Insight, Narrative, and Recommendation — must be fully traceable back to the real evidence and sources that produced it. Intelligence is never permitted to appear as a black box.
- **Reasoning:** A conclusion a user cannot trace is a conclusion they can only accept or reject on faith, and a platform whose entire premise is trustworthy intelligence cannot ask for that kind of faith. This principle is the unifying, non-negotiable form of what Design Principles 1 ("No Insight Without Evidence") and 2 ("No Recommendation Without Explanation") already require separately — those name two halves of the same guarantee; this names the whole of it.
- **Real-world example:** A user who distrusts a Health Score can always follow it backward — through the Insight that produced it, to the Evidence behind that Insight, to the Source the Evidence came from — and never hits a dead end.
- **Expected user outcome:** No conclusion in Base Radar is ever a black box; every one can be inspected down to its origin.

**The Traceability Chain.** Every intelligence output can be traced through four layers:

```
Recommendation
   ↓
Insight
   ↓
Evidence
   ↓
Source
```

- **Recommendation** — the action or guidance presented to the user.
- **Insight** — the interpretation generated from one or more validated signals.
- **Evidence** — the factual observations supporting the Insight.
- **Source** — the original origin of the Evidence: on-chain data, official announcements, governance proposals, GitHub activity, protocol documentation, or another trusted source, per the [Source Framework](#source-framework) below.

This is a reader-facing summary of the same guarantee [03. Information Architecture](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#ia-principles)'s own "Every Recommendation Is Traceable" principle already establishes architecturally, and of this chapter's own, more granular [Intelligence Pipeline](#intelligence-pipeline) below — "Evidence" here is what that pipeline's Signal Detection, Context, and Verification stages actually produce. All three descriptions are the same guarantee at three different levels of detail, never three competing claims.

**Why this matters.** Traceability is what makes every other promise in this chapter possible rather than aspirational: **Trust** is only durable when it can be checked, not just asserted. **Transparency** means nothing if a user can see *that* a conclusion exists but never *why*. **Explainability** (the [Explainability Framework](#explainability-framework) below) is this principle's direct consequence, not a separate concern. **User confidence** grows from a user's own ability to verify a claim, not from the Platform's insistence that it's correct. **Decision support** requires a user to weigh a Recommendation's real basis, which is impossible without a trace to weigh it against. **Auditability** means anyone, including Base Radar itself, can check whether a past conclusion held up, because the chain that produced it was never discarded. It also directly **reduces the risk of AI hallucination** — a system required to show its chain back to a real Source has nowhere to hide a fabricated claim — and it is what lets users **verify conclusions independently** rather than trust the Platform's authority alone, making **black-box intelligence** (see [Anti-Patterns](#anti-patterns) below) structurally impossible rather than merely discouraged.

**Relationship to other Product Bible chapters:**
- **[03. Information Architecture](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#information-ownership)** — Information Ownership ensures every piece of information has exactly one canonical owner; Traceable Intelligence is what makes that ownership actually findable from wherever a Recommendation happens to be displayed.
- **[02. UX Strategy](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#core-ux-principles)** — the user experience must always make Evidence and Confidence visible; this principle is why there is always something real for that visibility to show.
- **[04. Feature Specifications](04_FEATURE_SPECIFICATIONS.md)** — every feature that surfaces intelligence must preserve this chain intact, never truncate it for a cleaner-looking screen.
- **[01. Product Strategy](01_PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md#product-principles)** — trust is earned through transparency; this principle is that value's non-negotiable form at the intelligence layer.

**Non-Goals:** This principle defines product philosophy only. It never defines an AI model, algorithm, confidence calculation, ranking formula, database schema, or other implementation detail — those remain entirely outside this chapter's scope, per its own opening rule.

## Intelligence Pipeline

Every piece of intelligence passes through ten conceptual stages before it reaches a user. This is conceptual only — no stage below describes an algorithm, a model, or an implementation:

```
Raw Data
   ↓
Normalization
   ↓
Verification
   ↓
Signal Detection
   ↓
Context
   ↓
Narrative Detection
   ↓
Scoring
   ↓
Insight Generation
   ↓
Recommendation
   ↓
Explanation
```

- **Raw Data** — what a source actually reports, unmodified.
- **Normalization** — raw data reshaped into a form comparable across sources and time.
- **Verification** — a check for internal consistency and plausibility before anything is built on top of it.
- **Signal Detection** — the point at which a normalized, verified observation becomes worth noting at all.
- **Context** — the signal is read against history, peers, or expectation — never presented alone.
- **Narrative Detection** — the contextualized signal is checked against the Platform's finite set of classifications (see [Narrative Framework](#narrative-framework)).
- **Scoring** — the signal and its context are synthesized into the Platform's finite Health/Risk/Confidence vocabulary.
- **Insight Generation** — a scored, classified read is assembled into a coherent statement about what's happening and why.
- **Recommendation** — the Insight is framed as something worth a user's attention, per the [Recommendation Framework](#recommendation-framework) below.
- **Explanation** — the full trace back through every prior stage is packaged so it's retrievable on demand, satisfying the [Explainability Framework](#explainability-framework).

This pipeline is the conceptual elaboration of [03. Information Architecture](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#intelligence-hierarchy)'s own, coarser-grained Intelligence Hierarchy (Raw Data → Processed Data → Signals → Insights → Recommendations → Actions): 03's "Processed Data" corresponds to this pipeline's Normalization and Verification together; 03's "Signals" corresponds to Signal Detection, Context, and Narrative Detection together; 03's "Insights" corresponds to Scoring and Insight Generation together. 03 owns the fact that these stages are structurally distinct and where each one's data lives; this chapter owns what conceptually happens at each one. This pipeline stops at Explanation rather than 03's final "Actions" stage deliberately — Explanation is the last thing the intelligence *pipeline* itself produces; Action is something the *user* does afterward, which is why it belongs to [03](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#intelligence-hierarchy)'s ownership, not this one's.

## Types of Intelligence

Ten domains organize every intelligence output. Each is a lens the pipeline above is applied through, not a separate pipeline of its own. The features that surface each domain are specified in [04. Feature Specifications](04_FEATURE_SPECIFICATIONS.md); this section defines the domain itself.

- **Market Intelligence** — *Purpose:* reads price, volume, and liquidity signal. *Questions answered:* what is the market actually doing? *Primary outputs:* trend and momentum classifications. *User value:* separates real market movement from noise.
- **Project Intelligence** — *Purpose:* synthesizes everything known about one Project into a single read. *Questions answered:* is this project healthy? *Primary outputs:* Health Score, Risk classification. *User value:* one trustworthy answer instead of a dozen scattered metrics.
- **Narrative Intelligence** — *Purpose:* classifies the pattern a Project or the ecosystem is currently exhibiting. *Questions answered:* what story is the evidence actually telling? *Primary outputs:* Narrative classifications. *User value:* pattern recognition a user would otherwise have to do manually across many data points.
- **Portfolio Intelligence** — *Purpose:* applies Project Intelligence across everything a specific user holds. *Questions answered:* how healthy is what I actually hold? *Primary outputs:* portfolio-level health and diversification synthesis. *User value:* a personalized read, not just an aggregate one.
- **Governance Intelligence** — *Purpose:* reads proposal state and participation timing. *Questions answered:* what's being decided, and does it affect me? *Primary outputs:* proposal summaries, participation reminders. *User value:* decision support without Base Radar deciding on the user's behalf.
- **Risk Intelligence** — *Purpose:* identifies and classifies what deserves caution. *Questions answered:* what could go wrong, and how likely is it? *Primary outputs:* Risk classifications and their supporting Evidence. *User value:* caution that's proportionate to real signal, never inflated for attention.
- **Developer Intelligence** — *Purpose:* reads engineering activity as a proxy for project health. *Questions answered:* is this still being actively built? *Primary outputs:* activity-based contributions to the Health Score. *User value:* a real, hard-to-fake signal distinct from market sentiment.
- **Community Intelligence** — *Purpose:* reads social and community engagement signal where it's genuinely available. *Questions answered:* is there real, active interest here? *Primary outputs:* community-activity contributions to Narrative and Health reads. *User value:* signal grounded in real engagement, honestly limited where no free, reliable source exists.
- **Capital Flow Intelligence** — *Purpose:* reads how value is moving between projects, protocols, and wallets. *Questions answered:* where is capital actually going? *Primary outputs:* flow-based contributions to Market and Risk Intelligence. *User value:* an early, evidence-based read on shifting attention before it becomes obvious elsewhere.
- **Ecosystem Intelligence** — *Purpose:* synthesizes every domain above into one ecosystem-wide read. *Questions answered:* what's happening across Base as a whole, right now? *Primary outputs:* the Daily Brief. *User value:* the single, front-loaded answer the Dashboard exists to deliver.

## Intelligence Hierarchy

This is a different axis from [03. Information Architecture](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#intelligence-hierarchy)'s same-named section and from this chapter's own [Intelligence Pipeline](#intelligence-pipeline) above. Where those describe *how a single piece of intelligence is produced*, this describes *how much a piece of intelligence has matured* as evidence accumulates around it over time:

```
Observation
   ↓
Signal
   ↓
Insight
   ↓
Recommendation
   ↓
Decision Support
```

- **Observation** — a single data point has been noted. Nothing is claimed about it yet.
- **Signal** — the observation has recurred or crossed a threshold meaningfully enough to be worth tracking.
- **Insight** — the signal has been given context and classified — a real, if still narrow, understanding exists.
- **Recommendation** — the insight has been framed as something actionable, with confidence and evidence attached.
- **Decision Support** — enough corroborating evidence, context, and confidence has accumulated that the intelligence is robust enough to genuinely inform a real decision.

Users should be able to see which stage a given piece of intelligence has actually reached, rather than have everything presented as equally mature — a fresh Observation and a well-evidenced piece of Decision Support are not the same kind of claim, and presenting them identically would be its own form of overstated confidence, in violation of Design Principle 3 above.

## Confidence Framework

Confidence is one of the most important ideas in this entire chapter, and the single easiest one to get wrong: **confidence is not certainty.** Confidence communicates evidence *quality* — how well-supported a conclusion is — never a guarantee that the conclusion is correct. A high-confidence Insight can still be wrong; it means the evidence available genuinely supported it, not that reality is guaranteed to match it.

Seven conceptual dimensions inform confidence. This section defines what each dimension *means* — never how it is weighted, combined, or computed:

- **Evidence Quality** — how direct and reliable the underlying data is, as opposed to inferred or approximated.
- **Source Reliability** — how trustworthy the specific source of a given signal has proven to be.
- **Freshness** — how recently the evidence was actually observed, per Design Principle 8 above.
- **Coverage** — how much of the relevant picture the available evidence actually spans, versus how much is a genuine blind spot.
- **Consensus** — whether multiple independent signals agree, versus resting on a single one.
- **Conflicting Evidence** — whether any signal actively disagrees with the conclusion, per Design Principle 7 above.
- **Unknowns** — what, specifically, isn't known yet, stated rather than silently absorbed into the score.

Confidence exists to guide a user's own judgment, never to replace it: a low-confidence Insight is not withheld, it is disclosed *as* low-confidence, so the user can decide how much weight it deserves — exactly the discipline [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#core-ux-principles)'s Core UX Principle 2 depends on this chapter to uphold beneath the surface.

## Trust Framework

Trust is earned, never assumed — the operating premise behind every principle below, several of which extend [01](01_PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md#product-principles)'s "transparency about sourcing" and [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#ai-experience)'s "never hallucinate, never overstate confidence" into concrete epistemic practice at the production layer:

- **Always expose sources.** No signal is presented without a retrievable path to what produced it.
- **Never hide uncertainty.** A genuinely uncertain conclusion is disclosed as uncertain, not smoothed into false confidence.
- **Never invent facts.** Absence of data is reported as absence, never filled in with a plausible guess (Design Principle 6).
- **Separate observations from conclusions.** What was directly seen and what was inferred from it are never presented as the same kind of statement.
- **Separate conclusions from recommendations.** What the evidence shows and what a user might do about it are two distinct claims, never merged into one.
- **Distinguish verified information from interpretation.** A fact confirmed by a primary source and a read synthesized from several signals are labeled differently, never given equal footing.
- **Explain limitations.** Where this framework's methodology has a genuine blind spot, that limitation is stated rather than papered over.
- **Admit missing information.** The honest absence of a data point is treated as legitimate output, not a failure to hide (per Design Principle 12).

## Recommendation Framework

Every Recommendation must be able to answer six questions on demand:

1. **Why am I seeing this?** — the trigger.
2. **What evidence supports this?** — the Evidence.
3. **How confident are we?** — the Confidence Framework's read.
4. **What are the risks?** — what could make this Recommendation wrong.
5. **What should I do next?** — an optional, never mandatory, implication.
6. **Where can I learn more?** — the path to the full Explanation and Sources.

A Recommendation that cannot answer all six is not a finished Recommendation. This checklist is what Design Principle 2 ("No Recommendation Without Explanation") and the [Explainability Framework](#explainability-framework) below actually require in practice — and question 6 in particular is Design Principle 14, [Traceable Intelligence](#intelligence-design-principles), made into a literal, answerable question: a Recommendation that cannot point somewhere real is not user-centered, it is unverifiable. Recommendations remain user-centered rather than directive because every one of these six questions is framed around what the *user* needs to evaluate the Recommendation, not around convincing the user to act — the moment a Recommendation stops being evaluable and starts being persuasive, it has crossed into the "opinion presented as fact" [anti-pattern](#anti-patterns) below.

## Narrative Framework

A Narrative is a classification of a pattern already present in the evidence — never a prediction, per Design Principle 5 above. Narratives differ from news in a specific way: news reports that something happened; a Narrative classifies *what kind* of pattern that event is part of, synthesized across many individual events rather than reporting any single one of them. A single funding announcement is news; "Development" as a sustained classification across months of commits, releases, and funding activity is a Narrative.

Narratives evolve as evidence accumulates or changes — a project classified as "Development" today can shift to "Growth" as adoption signal appears, or to "Decline" as activity falls away; a Narrative is always a read on the *current* body of evidence, never a fixed label. Narratives influence Projects by becoming part of that Project's own Intelligence read (see [03](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#product-hierarchy)'s Intelligence level); they influence Portfolios by aggregating across everything a user holds into a portfolio-level synthesis, per [04](04_FEATURE_SPECIFICATIONS.md#portfolio)'s Portfolio capabilities. Narratives matter because they are the mechanism that turns individually unremarkable signals into a genuinely useful pattern — the entire justification for [01](01_PRODUCT_STRATEGY.md#product-philosophy)'s claim that Base Radar does the triage work of separating signal from noise, rather than leaving that work to the user.

## Evidence Framework

Not all evidence carries the same weight. Five categories exist:

- **Primary Sources** — direct, first-party data (on-chain state, a project's own published contracts).
- **Secondary Sources** — data reported about a primary source by another real system (a provider's aggregation of on-chain activity).
- **Derived Signals** — a new observation computed from combining primary or secondary sources (a trend derived from a time series).
- **Supporting Evidence** — additional signals that corroborate a conclusion without being its primary basis.
- **Contradictory Evidence** — signals that disagree with a conclusion, always retained and disclosed per Design Principle 7, never discarded because they're inconvenient.

The hierarchy runs in that order: a conclusion resting on Primary Sources carries more inherent weight than one resting only on Derived Signals, and this ordering is itself one of the [Confidence Framework](#confidence-framework)'s Evidence Quality dimension's inputs.

## Source Framework

Eight categories of real-world source exist, each with its own strengths and limitations. This is a different axis from [03](03_INFORMATION_ARCHITECTURE.md#taxonomy)'s Source Types (Live, Fallback, Inferred, which describe a source's *freshness/directness*) — this describes *what kind* of real-world source it is:

- **Official Sources** — a project's own published materials. *Strength:* authoritative on identity and intent. *Limitation:* self-reported, not independently verified.
- **On-chain Data** — the blockchain itself. *Strength:* cannot be faked or retracted. *Limitation:* reveals only what happened, never why.
- **Governance** — proposal and voting records. *Strength:* a real, binding decision record. *Limitation:* reflects only what was formally proposed, not the full space of relevant activity.
- **GitHub** — public development activity. *Strength:* hard to fabricate at scale. *Limitation:* activity volume is not the same as activity quality.
- **Security Researchers** — independent audits and disclosures. *Strength:* adversarial, rigorous scrutiny. *Limitation:* coverage is uneven — absence of a finding is not proof of absence of a problem.
- **Protocol Teams** — direct communication from the people building a project. *Strength:* firsthand intent and context. *Limitation:* inherently interested, not neutral.
- **Independent Analysts** — third-party research and commentary. *Strength:* an outside perspective official sources can't provide. *Limitation:* quality and rigor vary widely between analysts.
- **Community** — public sentiment and discussion. *Strength:* an early, organic signal. *Limitation:* the easiest category to manipulate at scale, and weighted accordingly.

## Explainability Framework

Every intelligence output must let a user understand five things, on demand:

- **Where intelligence came from** — which real sources produced it.
- **Why the conclusion exists** — the reasoning chain from Evidence to Insight.
- **What evidence supports it** — the specific signals behind it.
- **What assumptions exist** — anything treated as given rather than directly observed.
- **What uncertainty exists** — everything the [Confidence Framework](#confidence-framework) flagged as unresolved.

This is the synthesis point for every framework above: Explainability is what the Evidence Framework, Source Framework, Confidence Framework, and Trust Framework exist to make possible on demand, and it is the final stage of the [Intelligence Pipeline](#intelligence-pipeline) for exactly that reason.

## Intelligence Quality Checklist

Every intelligence output should be:

- **Explainable** — because a conclusion no one can account for cannot be trusted, only obeyed.
- **Verifiable** — because a claim that can't be checked against real evidence is indistinguishable from a claim that was made up.
- **Traceable** — because a Recommendation must be followable back to its Source, per Design Principle 1.
- **Actionable** — because information without implication is data, not intelligence, per the Intelligence Philosophy above.
- **Relevant** — because intelligence a user has no reason to care about is noise regardless of how well-evidenced it is.
- **Timely** — because stale intelligence presented as current is a form of dishonesty, per Design Principle 8.
- **Confidence Qualified** — because an unqualified conclusion overstates what the evidence actually supports.
- **Source Attributed** — because trust is earned through traceability, not asserted.
- **Context Aware** — because a fact without context is not yet intelligence, per the Intelligence Philosophy above.
- **User Centered** — because the decision always belongs to the user, never to the Platform.

## Anti-Patterns

Base Radar's intelligence never adopts any of the following:

- **Opinion presented as fact** — violates Evidence Before Opinion.
- **Hidden confidence** — violates Confidence over Assumption and Core UX Principle 2.
- **Black-box recommendations** — violates No Recommendation Without Explanation.
- **Clickbait intelligence** — a conclusion sensationalized beyond what its evidence and confidence actually support.
- **Unverifiable claims** — anything presented as fact with no retrievable Source.
- **Fear-based recommendations** — urgency manufactured beyond what real risk evidence supports, the inverse of the honest severity-matching [02](02_UX_STRATEGY.md#ux-writing-principles) already requires in tone.
- **Information overload** — volume presented in place of synthesis, the opposite of what Signal over Noise demands.
- **Unexplained scores** — any Health, Risk, or Confidence value shown with no path to what produced it.
- **Marketing disguised as intelligence** — promotional language presented with the neutral authority of an evidence-based read.

## Future Intelligence Vision

This framework's philosophy — not its implementation — is what has to hold as Base Radar's intelligence platform grows. As [07. Engineering Roadmap](07_ENGINEERING_ROADMAP.md)'s Releases extend real identity and real cross-device sync, intelligence itself becomes portable across a user's devices without becoming less accountable — the same Explainability and Confidence guarantees apply whether an Insight is read on a phone or a desktop, today or after a sync. As [09. Product Backlog](09_PRODUCT_BACKLOG.md)'s more ambitious AI ideas mature — natural-language Q&A over existing Intelligence output, exposed narrative-classification thresholds — each is explicitly gated by this chapter's own rules before it can graduate: a conversational layer is only acceptable once it can answer strictly from already-computed, Evidence-backed Insight, per Design Principle 1, never as a freeform generative layer that could fabricate a claim this framework has no Evidence for. The philosophy does not loosen as the platform's capability grows — capability only ever grows into what the philosophy already permits.
